First Principles

My approach to innovation, from a high school soda business to optimizing GPU drivers. Click to read the story.

Identify. Deconstruct. Build.

In high school, I saw two problems: I had no money, and our campus banned soda.

Instead of just breaking the rules, I broke down the problem. What *is* soda? Carbonated water, sugar, and flavor. I could source all of those. So I dove into the science, experimenting with sugar inversion ratios for mouthfeel and osmotic maceration to extract flavors from raw mint, ginger, and roots. I wasn't just making soda; I was engineering a flavor system from scratch.

The result was a product so good, the administration gave me a permit to sell it—on one condition: teachers got theirs for free. I learned a key lesson: when you understand a system from its first principles, you can rewrite the rules.

This mindset is how I approach everything, from a soda business to optimizing a GPU driver cache at NVIDIA. It’s about finding the core truth of a problem and building the most effective solution, whether for flavor or for performance.

NVIDIA: Architecting Beyond the Code

During a routine debug of a Linux graphics driver, I noticed a performance bottleneck that wasn't explained by the code logic. Standard procedure was to patch the driver, but I suspected a deeper issue.

After getting permission to investigate further, I traced the issue to a major L2 cache inefficiency in how the firmware handled data pipelines. By reframing the problem from "fixing a bug" to "optimizing the architecture," I was able to propose a new data flow that not only fixed the bug but improved overall performance in related tasks. This experience taught me to always question the underlying assumptions of a system.

Microsoft: Building Trust from Scratch

The task was to integrate a standard cryptographic verification library into Windows Cloud Infrastructure. However, relying on existing black-box solutions didn't sit right with the FIPS compliance requirements and our need for absolute certainty.

Starting from first principles, I engineered a new, lightweight cryptographic library in Rust. This involved deep dives into X.509 Digital Signature standards and the complexities of ASN.1 data parsing. Building it from the ground up ensured not only perfect compliance but also a more performant and auditable component, reducing handshake latency by 10% for millions of transactions.

My Innovation Playbook

Core Philosophy: Attack the Monkey First

If your goal is to teach a monkey to recite Shakespeare on a pedestal, most people start by building the pedestal. I start by training the monkey. In any ambitious project, there is one core challenge that holds all the risk—the 'monkey'. My philosophy is to attack that single, hardest part of the problem first. We de-risk the entire venture by proving the 'impossible' is possible before we write a single line of code for the easy parts.

The Framework for Groundbreaking Work

  • Who is this for? (Defining the human problem)
  • Why are we making this? (Defining the core mission)
  • Why hasn't this been done? (Finding the unique advantage)
  • Where is the impact? (Identifying the scale of the opportunity)
  • How does this change the world? (Articulating the vision)

For well-defined projects, we play by the rules and execute with discipline. Knowing which approach to use is the key.

Proof in Practice: The Innovation Collective

To put this playbook into practice, I founded and led the 'Innovation Collective'—a curated group of the sharpest minds from UCSC, Stanford, and Berkeley across a range of disciplines: History, Physics, Mechanical Engineering, and Computer Science. It was my lab for seeing how brilliant, diverse minds could deconstruct and solve complex problems together. This is how I build the teams that build the future.
